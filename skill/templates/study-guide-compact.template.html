Chapter 9 · Meiosis
§1Sex chromosomes: humans have 22 pairs of autosomes + 1 pair sex chromosomes. XX = female, XY = male. Y carries SRY → male development. Father determines sex.
§2Meiosis math: 1 round of DNA replication (S phase) + 2 rounds of division → 4 haploid gametes from 1 diploid cell. No replication between meiosis I and II.
§3Meiosis I (reductive): separates homologous pairs. Sister chromatids stay attached. 2n → n. Each daughter has duplicated chromosomes.
§4Meiosis II (equational): separates sister chromatids. Mechanically like mitosis but starting haploid. Final result: 4 haploid cells with single chromosomes.
§5Prophase I: homologues pair (synapsis); §6 crossing over occurs at chiasmata; nuclear envelope breaks; spindle forms. Longest phase.
§6Crossing over: exchange of DNA between non-sister chromatids of homologues during §5. 2–3 chiasmata per pair in humans. Produces genetic recombination (new allele combinations).
§7Metaphase I: homologous pairs line up at equator with random orientation per pair → independent assortment. 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million combos in humans.
§8Anaphase I vs II: Ana I separates homologues (chromatids stay together). Ana II separates sister chromatids. Mitotic anaphase = like ana II.
§9Independent assortment: alleles for one gene segregate into gametes independently of alleles for a different gene. Cellular basis = random metaphase I orientation. Only true for genes on different chromosomes (or far apart). See §23.
§10Nondisjunction = chromosomes fail to separate in anaphase. In meiosis I: ALL 4 gametes abnormal (2 extra, 2 missing). In meiosis II: 2 abnormal + 2 normal. Trisomy 21 → Down syndrome.

§12Why sex? Genetic diversity → some offspring survive when conditions change. Asexual reproduction = clones, vulnerable to single threats but faster.
§13Twins: Monozygotic (identical) = one zygote splits, identical DNA. Dizygotic (fraternal) = two eggs, two sperm; ~50% shared DNA like ordinary siblings.
Chapter 10 · Patterns of Inheritance
§14Vocabulary fast:
  • Allele: alternative version of a gene
  • Genotype: two alleles (YY, Yy, yy)
  • Phenotype: observable trait
  • Homozygous: same alleles (YY/yy)
  • Heterozygous: different (Yy)
  • Carrier: heterozygous unaffected
  • Dominant: shows when present
  • Recessive: masked by dominant
§15True breeding = pure breeding = homozygous. Self-fertilized offspring all match the parent's trait.
§16P / F1 / F2: P = true-breeding parents. F1 = hybrid offspring (all dominant phenotype if YY × yy). F2 = F1 self-cross → 3:1 phenotype, 1:2:1 genotype.
§17Mendel's law of segregation: the two alleles for a gene separate during meiosis. Gametes carry only one. Cellular basis = anaphase I.
§18Punnett squares: Aa × Aa → 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa → 3 dominant : 1 recessive (with full dominance). Heterozygote gametes: 1/2 A, 1/2 a.
§19Incomplete dominance = heterozygote shows blended intermediate (red × white → pink). Genotype:phenotype ratio 1:2:1.
§20Codominance = both alleles fully expressed simultaneously, NOT blended. Example §21.
§21ABO blood typing: alleles I^A, I^B, i. I^A and I^B are codominant; both dominant over i. I^A I^B → AB (both antigens). ii → O (none).
§22Sex-linked recessive: trait on X chromosome. Males (XY) only need 1 copy → expressed. Females (XX) need 2 → carriers possible. Affected mom + normal dad → all daughters carriers, all sons affected.
§23Gene linkage = genes close together on same chromosome inherited as a unit (exception to §9). §6 Crossing over breaks linkage; probability ∝ distance.
§24X-inactivation = in each XX cell, ONE X is randomly silenced (Barr body). Prevents double-dosing. Why calicos have patches; why Rett syndrome severity varies in girls.
§25Polygenic = ONE trait, MANY genes (skin, height) → continuous variation. Pleiotropy = ONE gene, MANY effects (Marfan: tall + lens + aorta). Don't confuse.
§26Environment + phenotype: Siamese cat fur — pigment enzyme only works at cool temps → dark on extremities. Genotype fixed; phenotype = genotype × environment.
Chapter 11 · DNA Structure & Replication
§27Nucleotide = 3 parts: phosphate (PO₄⁻) + deoxyribose sugar + nitrogenous base. Backbone faces exterior; bases face interior of helix.
§28Bases & pairing: A–T via 2 H-bonds, G–C via 3 H-bonds. Purines (A, G) pair with pyrimidines (T, C) — equal helix width. RNA: U replaces T.
§29Chargaff's rule: %A = %T and %G = %C. Worked example: if T = 20%, then A = 20%, G = C = 30% each.
§30Antiparallel: the two strands run in opposite 5'→3' directions. Why? DNA pol can only build 5'→3'; antiparallel template forces leading/lagging strand asymmetry §37.
§315' vs 3' end: 5' carbon carries phosphate. 3' carbon carries free –OH. DNA pol extends only at 3'-OH → needs primer.
§32Complement example: template 3'-TACGGT-5' → mRNA 5'-AUGCCA-3'. (For DNA→DNA: substitute T for U.) Antiparallel + complementary.
§33Cell cycle: G1 → S phase (DNA replication) → G2 → M (mitosis or meiosis). DNA replicates ONCE per cycle.
§34Semiconservative replication: each daughter helix has 1 old strand + 1 new strand. Confirmed by Meselson-Stahl (1958).
§35Replication enzymes:
  • Helicase — unwinds helix (breaks H-bonds)
  • SSB — stabilizes single strands
  • Topoisomerase — relieves twist tension
  • Primase — lays RNA primer (3'-OH start)
  • DNA pol III — extends 5'→3', proofreads
  • DNA pol I — replaces RNA primers w/ DNA
  • Ligase — seals fragment gaps
Mnemonic: "Helicase Pulls Apart, Primer Provides A 3' Start, Pol III Adds the Parts, Pol I Rewrites RNA, Ligase Seals" (HPPPL).
§36Direction of synthesis: template read 3'→5'; new strand built 5'→3'. The 3'-OH on the existing strand is what gets extended.

§40Telomeres: repetitive DNA (TTAGGG) at chromosome ends. Linear chromosomes shorten each replication because lagging strand can't extend the very end (no upstream 3'-OH after primer removal).
§41Telomerase: enzyme that ADDS telomere repeats using its own RNA template. Active in germ cells, stem cells, ~85% of cancers. Inactive in most somatic cells → telomere shortening with age.
§42Replication accuracy: raw error ~1 / 1000 nt. After DNA pol III proofreading: ~1 / 10⁷ nt.
§43Mutation = permanent change in DNA. Most occur during replication. Mutagen = agent that increases mutation rate (UV, X-rays, chemicals). Types: §66
§44Same mechanism mitosis vs meiosis: DNA replicates the same way regardless of which division follows. The difference is in the divisions, not the replication.
§45Griffith (1928): heat-killed virulent S bacteria + live harmless R bacteria → live virulent S recovered. Something in dead S "transformed" R. Later identified as DNA (Avery et al. 1944).
Chapter 12 · How Genes Work — Transcription & Translation
§46Central dogma: DNA → (transcription) → RNA → (translation) → Protein. Transcription in nucleus; translation at ribosomes in cytoplasm.
§47RNA vs DNA — 3 differences:

§48Three RNA classes:
  • mRNA — message; codons read at ribosome
  • tRNA — adaptor; anticodon ↔ codon, carries amino acid
  • rRNA — ribosome structure + catalysis (peptide bonds). Ribosomes are ribozymes.
§49Transcription steps:
  • Initiation: RNA polymerase binds promoter (eukaryotes need transcription factors first); unwinds DNA
  • Elongation: reads template 3'→5'; builds RNA 5'→3'; only 1 strand copied
  • Termination: at terminator sequence, all 3 (RNA, DNA, polymerase) separate
§50mRNA processing (eukaryotes, in nucleus):
  • 5' cap — modified G; ribosome recognition + protection
  • 3' poly-A tail — ~100–250 As; stability + export
  • Splicing — introns removed, exons joined
§51Introns vs exons:
  • Exons stay = EXpressed (in mature mRNA, code for protein)
  • Introns go = INtervening (spliced out)
Alternative splicing → multiple proteins from one gene.
§52Codon = 3-nt sequence on mRNA → 1 amino acid. 4³ = 64 codons. 61 code amino acids; 3 stop codons.
§53Start codon = AUG → methionine (Met). EVERY protein begins with Met (sometimes cleaved later). Initiator tRNA carries Met.
§54Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA. No tRNA matches. Release factor binds A site → polypeptide released → ribosome dissociates.
Mnemonic: U Are Away · U Are Gone · U Go Away.
§55Codon redundancy: 64 codons / 20 amino acids → most amino acids have multiple codons (esp. 3rd position "wobble"). Why some substitutions are silent.
§56Translation steps:
  • Initiation: small subunit binds 5' cap, scans to AUG; initiator tRNA(Met) base-pairs; large subunit joins → ribosome assembled
  • Elongation: 2nd tRNA enters A site (matches codon); rRNA forms peptide bond; 1st tRNA exits via E site; ribosome shifts; repeat
  • Termination: stop codon → release factor → done
§57Ribosome sites: A (Aminoacyl, incoming) → P (Peptidyl, growing chain) → E (Exit). tRNAs flow A → P → E.
§58Where catalysis happens: rRNA in the large subunit forms the peptide bond — ribosomes are ribozymes (RNA enzymes), not protein enzymes.
§59Polyribosomes: multiple ribosomes can translate the same mRNA at the same time → fast protein production from one transcript.

§61Eukaryotic gene regulation (6 levels):
  • Chromatin (histone packing)
  • Transcription factors
  • Alternative splicing
  • mRNA export from nucleus
  • mRNA stability + microRNA silencing
  • Post-translational (folding, modification)
§62Transcription factors: proteins that must bind near promoter for RNA polymerase to engage in eukaryotes. Different cell types → different TFs → different gene sets.
§63microRNA (miRNA): short non-coding RNA that base-pairs with mRNA to block translation or trigger degradation. Major post-transcriptional regulator.
§64Pre-mRNA = initial transcript. Longer than mature mRNA (still has introns + no cap/tail). Becomes mature mRNA after §50 processing.
§65Anticodon: 3-nt on tRNA that pairs with mRNA codon. Each tRNA carries one specific amino acid matched to its anticodon.
§66Mutation effects on protein:
  • Silent substitution: codon → same aa (codon redundancy)
  • Missense: codon → different aa (sickle cell)
  • Nonsense: codon → stop (truncated protein)
  • In-frame indel: adds/removes whole codons; mild
  • Frameshift: indel ≠ multiple of 3 → all downstream codons wrong → catastrophic
§67Sickle cell anemia: single A→T substitution in β-hemoglobin gene. GAG → GTG changes 6th amino acid Glu → Val. Hydrophobic Val causes hemoglobin to aggregate under low O₂; cells deform.
Chapter 13 · The New Biology — Biotechnology
§68Biotechnology = use of organisms or biomolecules for human purposes. Genetic engineering = subset that DIRECTLY alters genomes (recombinant DNA, CRISPR).
§69PCR (Mullis, Nobel 1993): amplifies a specific DNA target exponentially. Each cycle DOUBLES copies. After n cycles → 2ⁿ. 30 cycles → ~10⁹ copies.
§70PCR — 4 ingredients:
  • Target DNA — template
  • Heat-stable polymerase (Taq) — survives 95°C
  • dNTPs — building blocks
  • Primers — flank target, define amplification region
Missing any → no product. §71
§71PCR — 3 temperature steps:
Step°CWhat
Denature95H-bonds break; strands separate
Anneal55Primers bind
Extend72Taq builds new DNA
§72Gel electrophoresis: separates DNA by size. DNA negatively charged → migrates toward + electrode. Smaller = faster through agarose mesh → travels FARTHER. Bands sorted by length.
§73Short Tandem Repeat (STR): short DNA sequence (2–6 bp) repeated head-to-tail. Repeat count varies between people. CODIS uses 13 STRs → match probability ~1 in a trillion.
§74DNA profiling workflow: collect DNA → PCR amplifies 13 STR loci → gel electrophoresis → band pattern = profile. Mismatch on even one STR locus → exclusion.
§75DNA sequencing (Sanger): normal nucleotides + dye-labeled chain-terminators in replication mix. Each position fragment is color-coded by its terminal base. Run on gel → read order from shortest to longest.
§76Human Genome Project: 1990 → 2003. Sequenced all 3 billion bases with 99.99% accuracy. Found ~19,949 genes; genes are only ~2% of the genome.
§77Recombinant DNA: joining DNA segments from different sources (e.g. human insulin gene → bacterial plasmid → bacterium produces human insulin).
§78Other tools: gene therapy (replace defective gene), cloning (Dolly 1996), preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD: screen embryos at 8-cell stage).

§81Punnett worked example (TT × tt): F1 = all Tt (100% tall). F2 (Tt × Tt) = 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt → 3 tall : 1 short.
§82Sex-linked Punnett (X^h X^h × X^H Y): All daughters X^H X^h (carriers, normal). All sons X^h Y (affected). §22

§87DNA → mRNA quick rules:
  • Read template 3'→5'
  • Substitute base: A↔U, T→A, G↔C
  • Write mRNA 5'→3'
§88mRNA → protein quick rules:
  • Read mRNA 5'→3' starting at AUG
  • Group into codons (3 nt each)
  • Use codon chart → amino acid
  • Stop at first UAA/UAG/UGA
§89Inheritance pattern decision tree:
  • Hetero shows blend? → incomplete dominance §19
  • Both alleles fully expressed? → codominance §20
  • More males affected? → X-linked recessive §22
  • Continuous variation? → polygenic §25
  • One gene, many effects? → pleiotropy §25
§90Don't confuse:
  • Polygenic ≠ pleiotropy — see §25
  • Codominance ≠ incomplete dom. — blended vs both-expressed
  • Sister chromatids ≠ homologues — see §79
  • Anaphase I ≠ Anaphase II — see §8
  • Introns ≠ exons — Ex stay, In go §51
§91Key numbers to know:
  • Human chromosomes: 2n = 46 (23 pairs)
  • Human gametes: n = 23
  • Codons: 64 (61 amino + 3 stop)
  • Amino acids: 20
  • STRs in CODIS: 13 → 1 in 10¹²
  • Genes in human genome: ~19,949 (~2%)
  • Genome size: ~3 × 10⁹ bases

Top mistakes to avoid on exam day: ① Confusing anaphase I (homologues) with anaphase II (chromatids). ② Saying "DNA pol can start from scratch" — it can't, primase makes a primer. ③ Mixing up codominance and incomplete dominance — codominance shows BOTH at once; incomplete BLENDS. ④ Forgetting that males CAN'T be carriers for X-linked recessive. ⑤ Frameshift is worse than substitution because EVERY downstream codon shifts.
