# How do different mutations affect proteins?

## Answer

| Mutation | Effect on codons | Possible protein effects |
|---|---|---|
| **Substitution (silent)** | One base swap changes a codon to another that codes for the **same amino acid** | None — protein unchanged. Possible thanks to **codon redundancy**. |
| **Substitution (missense)** | One base swap changes one codon to a different amino acid | Mild to severe depending on which amino acid and where. **Sickle cell anemia** is a missense (Glu → Val in hemoglobin). |
| **Substitution (nonsense)** | Codon → stop codon | **Truncated, often non-functional** protein |
| **Insertion (in-frame, multiple of 3)** | Adds one or more codons | Extra amino acid(s); usually mild |
| **Deletion (in-frame, multiple of 3)** | Removes one or more codons | Missing amino acid(s); usually mild |
| **Insertion or deletion NOT a multiple of 3 → Frameshift** | Reading frame shifts; every downstream codon changes | **Catastrophic** — usually produces a non-functional protein and often a premature stop codon |

[[diseases/sickle-cell-anemia]] is the textbook example: a single A→T substitution changes the 6th amino acid of β-hemoglobin from glutamate to valine, causing red blood cells to deform under low oxygen.
