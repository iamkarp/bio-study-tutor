# What did Mendel observe and why?

## Answer
Mendel crossed two **true-breeding** parents differing in one trait (e.g. purple-flowered PP × white-flowered pp).

**F1 observation**: all offspring showed the **dominant** trait (all purple). _Why?_ Each parent contributed one allele; F1 are heterozygous (Pp), and the dominant allele masks the recessive.

**F2 observation**: when F1 self-pollinated (Pp × Pp), the recessive trait **reappeared** in roughly **3:1** dominant:recessive (e.g. 705 purple to 224 white). _Why?_ Each Pp parent makes two kinds of gametes (P or p) in equal numbers; the four possible offspring combinations (PP, Pp, Pp, pp) yield genotype ratio 1:2:1 and phenotype ratio 3:1.

**Principle**: alleles segregate into separate gametes during meiosis ([[principles/law-of-segregation]]), and they recombine randomly at fertilization. A recessive allele is not "diluted" — it can hide in a heterozygote and reappear in later generations.
