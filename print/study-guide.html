Chapter 9 — Sexual Reproduction and Meiosis

Summary

Chapter 9 introduces sexual reproduction as a source of genetic diversity, contrasts autosomes with sex chromosomes (XX female / XY male), then walks through meiosis: one round of DNA replication followed by two rounds of cell division (meiosis I and meiosis II) that produce four haploid gametes. §3Describe prophase I, metaphase I, anaphase I, and telophase I in detailExam topic

Describe the four phases of meiosis I

Answer

  1. Prophase I — Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis); chiasmata form where non-sister chromatids exchange DNA segments (Crossing Over[§18] → genetic recombination). Spindle microtubules assemble; nuclear envelope breaks down. This is the longest and most distinctive phase.
  2. Metaphase I — Paired homologous chromosomes line up at the cell equator. Each homologue is attached to the opposite pole by spindle microtubules. Maternal/paternal orientation is random — this is where Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment[§15] takes effect.
  3. Anaphase I — Homologous chromosomes (not sister chromatids!) separate and move to opposite poles. Each pole gets one duplicated member from each homologous pair. This is the key difference from mitotic anaphase.
  4. Telophase I — Two haploid clusters of duplicated chromosomes form. Spindle disappears; cytokinesis splits the cell. Nuclear envelopes may re-form. Chromosomes do not decondense or replicate before meiosis II.

§4Describe prophase II, metaphase II, anaphase II, and telophase II in detailExam topic

Describe the four phases of meiosis II

Answer

Meiosis II is mechanically very similar to mitosis (it separates sister chromatids), except it starts with haploid cells whose chromosomes are still duplicated.

  1. Prophase II — Chromosomes recondense if they had decondensed; spindle microtubules re-form and capture duplicated chromosomes; kinetochores of sister chromatids attach to opposite poles. No crossing over.
  2. Metaphase II — Duplicated chromosomes line up singly (not in pairs) at the equator, perpendicular to the spindle.
  3. Anaphase II — Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
  4. Telophase II — Cytokinesis occurs; nuclear membranes re-form; chromosomes decondense. The two nuclear divisions of meiosis ultimately produce four haploid cells from one diploid cell.

§5Explain the entire process of meiosis end-to-endExam topic

Explain the entire process of meiosis

Answer

Starting cell: 1 diploid cell with 2n chromosomes (e.g. 46 in humans). DNA has been replicated once during S phase of interphase, so each chromosome consists of two sister chromatids.

Meiosis I — reductive division (separates homologous pairs): 1. Prophase I — Homologues pair up; crossing over at chiasmata creates recombinant chromatids. Nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle forms. 2. Metaphase I — Homologous pairs line up at the equator; orientation of each pair is independent (independent assortment). 3. Anaphase I — Homologues separate; sister chromatids stay together. 4. Telophase I — Two haploid cells form, each with n duplicated chromosomes.

Meiosis II — equational division (separates sister chromatids; no DNA replication between): 5. Prophase II — Spindle re-forms. 6. Metaphase II — Duplicated chromosomes line up singly. 7. Anaphase II — Sister chromatids separate. 8. Telophase II — Four haploid cells form, each with n single chromosomes.

End result: 1 diploid cell → 4 genetically distinct haploid gametes, each carrying a unique mixture of maternal and paternal alleles thanks to crossing over and independent assortment.

§6How does sexual reproduction improve survival of ecological disasters?Exam topic

How does sexual reproduction improve survival of ecological disasters?

Answer

Sexual reproduction produces genetic diversity among offspring. When the environment changes (disease outbreak, climate shift, habitat disruption), at least some offspring are likely to carry allele combinations that allow them to survive — diversity hedges against catastrophe. Asexually-reproducing populations are clones; one lethal stressor can wipe them all out.

How many rounds of cell division occur in meiosis?

Answer

Two rounds of cell division. Meiosis I separates homologous pairs (reductive division: 2n → n); meiosis II separates sister chromatids (equational division: still n). The net result is 1 diploid cell → 4 haploid cells.

§9Outcome of meiosis I — if a cell starts with 23 pairs and replicates, what happens?Exam topic

Outcome of meiosis I

Answer

Meiosis I separates homologous pairs of chromosomes.

Worked example — start with 23 pairs (= 46 chromosomes, diploid). After DNA replication in S phase, you still have 46 chromosomes but each chromosome now consists of two sister chromatids. - After meiosis I: 2 daughter cells, each with 23 chromosomes (each still as sister chromatids — duplicated, but only one of each homologous pair).

The cells are haploid (n = 23) in chromosome count, but DNA content per cell equals 2× the haploid (because chromatids haven't separated yet).

§10Outcome of meiosis II — if a cell ends with 20 sister chromatid pairs, what happens?Exam topic

Outcome of meiosis II

Answer

Meiosis II separates sister chromatids. Each starting cell from meiosis I produces 2 daughter cells, so meiosis II as a whole produces 4 haploid cells from the 2 cells that finished meiosis I.

Worked example — a cell entering meiosis II has 20 sister-chromatid pairs (i.e. 20 duplicated chromosomes). - After meiosis II: 2 daughter cells per starting cell, each with 20 single chromosomes (chromatids now separated).

If two cells entered meiosis II (the products of one meiosis I), the final result is 4 cells × 20 chromosomes each.

§12What is crossing over and when does it occur? What's the point?Exam topic

What is crossing over and when does it occur?

Answer

Crossing over is the exchange of DNA segments between two homologous chromosomes that occurs during prophase I of meiosis. The exchange happens at sites called chiasmata (typically 2–3 per pair in human cells).

The point: crossing over generates genetic recombination — new allele combinations that did not exist on either parental chromosome. Combined with Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment[§15], it is the major source of genetic variation among gametes (and therefore among offspring).

§13What is nondisjunction? Outcome in meiosis I vs. meiosis II?Exam topic

What is nondisjunction?

Answer

Nondisjunction is the failure of chromosomes to separate properly during anaphase. It produces gametes with abnormal chromosome counts (an extra copy or a missing copy → aneuploidy).

  • Nondisjunction in meiosis I (homologues fail to separate): all four resulting gametes are abnormal — 2 with an extra chromosome, 2 missing one.
  • Nondisjunction in meiosis II (sister chromatids fail to separate): only the affected meiosis-I daughter cell makes abnormal gametes — 1 with an extra chromosome, 1 missing one, and 2 normal gametes.

A trisomy (extra chromosome) such as trisomy 21 causes Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)[§40]. Missing chromosomes are usually lethal before birth.

§14What is the law of independent assortment?Exam topic

What is the law of independent assortment?

Answer

The alleles of one gene segregate into gametes independently of the alleles of another gene.

In meiosis I, each homologous pair lines up at the metaphase plate independently of every other pair — maternal and paternal homologues end up on each pole at random. For 23 pairs of human chromosomes, this gives 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million possible chromosome combinations per gamete (before considering crossing over).

This is what Mendel's dihybrid cross[§69] revealed: a 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio in the F2 generation showed seed shape and seed color sort independently.

§15Mendel's Law of Independent AssortmentPrinciple

Law of Independent Assortment (Mendel's Second Law)

Summary

The alleles for one gene segregate into gametes independently of the alleles for another gene.

Cellular mechanism: at Metaphase I[§22], each homologous pair orients randomly on the spindle relative to every other pair. With 23 pairs of chromosomes, this gives 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million unique chromosome combinations per gamete (before considering crossing over).

Caveat: only true for genes on different chromosomes (or genes far apart on the same chromosome). Genes that sit close together on the same chromosome are linked[§66] and tend to be inherited together — though Crossing Over[§18] can break that linkage.

§18Crossing OverProcesse

Crossing Over

Summary

The exchange of DNA segments between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis. The exchange occurs at sites called chiasmata (typically 2–3 per pair in humans). When the exchanged segments carry different alleles, genetic recombination has occurred — chromatids now carry combinations of alleles never seen on either parental chromosome.

Why It Matters

Crossing over is one of two major sources of genetic variation in meiosis (the other is independent assortment[§15]). It also breaks up What is gene linkage?[§66] — closely linked genes are still usually inherited together, but the further apart two genes are, the more likely a crossover separates them.

§19MeiosisProcesse

Meiosis

Summary

Meiosis is the form of cell division that produces haploid gametes (sperm and eggs) from a diploid parent cell. One round of DNA replication is followed by two rounds of division — meiosis I (separates homologues) and meiosis II (separates sister chromatids) — yielding four genetically unique haploid daughter cells from one diploid starting cell.

Key Facts

  • DNA replicates once during S Phase[§132] of interphase before meiosis I.
  • No DNA replication occurs between meiosis I and meiosis II.
  • Sources of genetic variability: Crossing Over[§18] (in prophase I) and random orientation of homologous pairs (Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment[§15] in metaphase I).
  • Errors include Nondisjunction[§25], which can cause Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)[§40].

Steps

  1. Interphase (DNA replication during S phase)
  2. Meiosis I[§20] — separates homologous pairs
  3. Meiosis II[§21] — separates sister chromatids
  4. Cytokinesis → 4 haploid cells

§20Meiosis IProcesse

Meiosis I

Summary

The first of meiosis's two divisions. Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes (one of each pair to each daughter cell), reducing chromosome number from diploid to haploid. Sister chromatids stay attached.

Steps

  1. Prophase I[§27] — homologues pair, Crossing Over[§18] occurs at chiasmata
  2. Metaphase I[§22] — pairs line up at the equator with random orientation (Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment[§15])
  3. Anaphase I[§16] — homologues separate (sister chromatids stay together)
  4. Telophase I[§30] — two haploid cells form, each with duplicated chromosomes

Outcome

2 haploid daughter cells, each with n duplicated chromosomes. (Starting from 2n=46 in humans, each daughter has 23 chromosomes still composed of two sister chromatids.)

§21Meiosis IIProcesse

Meiosis II

Summary

The second meiotic division. Meiosis II separates sister chromatids (mechanically similar to mitosis) but starts from haploid cells already produced by meiosis I. No DNA replication between meiosis I and meiosis II.

Steps

  1. Prophase II[§28] — spindle re-forms; chromatids attach to opposite poles
  2. Metaphase II[§23] — duplicated chromosomes line up singly at the equator
  3. Anaphase II[§17] — sister chromatids separate
  4. Telophase II[§31] — four haploid cells form, each with single (unduplicated) chromosomes

Outcome

4 haploid cells total (across the two cells from meiosis I), each genetically unique due to crossing over and independent assortment in meiosis I.

§25NondisjunctionProcesse

Nondisjunction

Summary

Failure of chromosomes to separate properly during anaphase of meiosis. Produces gametes with extra or missing chromosomes (aneuploidy). When such a gamete fertilizes a normal one, the resulting zygote has a chromosomal disorder (e.g. trisomy 21 → Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)[§40]).

Outcomes

  • In meiosis I — homologues fail to separate. All four resulting gametes are abnormal: 2 with an extra chromosome, 2 missing one.
  • In meiosis II — sister chromatids fail to separate (in one of the two cells from meiosis I). Two of the four gametes are abnormal (1 extra, 1 missing); the other two are normal.

Most aneuploidies are lethal before birth. Notable exceptions: trisomy 21, trisomy 13 (Patau), trisomy 18 (Edwards), and sex-chromosome aneuploidies (XXY, XO, XYY).

§26OogenesisProcesse

Oogenesis

Summary

Meiosis in females. A diploid oogonium undergoes asymmetric divisions: cytokinesis is unequal, so most of the cytoplasm goes to one daughter cell. Result: one large egg (ovum) and 2–3 small polar bodies (which usually die). Begins before birth and pauses; one egg per menstrual cycle finishes meiosis if fertilized.

§45Why Sexual ReproductionConcept

Why Sexual Reproduction?

Summary

Sexual reproduction produces genetic diversity among offspring. Diversity provides a hedge: in a changing environment, at least some offspring are likely to carry combinations of alleles that allow them to survive disease, climate change, predator pressure, etc.

Disadvantages (also covered)

  • Only half of an individual's alleles get passed on (vs. 100% for asexual).
  • It takes time and energy to find a partner.
  • It can be risky.
  • Opportunities for mistakes (e.g. nondisjunction).

Asexual reproduction is faster and conserves the parent's full genome — but a population of clones has no buffer against new threats.

Diploid (2n)

Gamete

§50Genetic RecombinationVocabulary Term

Genetic Recombination

Summary

The production of new combinations of alleles in offspring. The two main sources during meiosis are Crossing Over[§18] (in prophase I) and independent assortment (in metaphase I). Together, they ensure that no two gametes from a single individual are identical (apart from rare exceptions).

§51Haploid (n)Vocabulary Term

Haploid (n)

Summary

A cell with one copy of each chromosome (one set, denoted n). Human haploid cells have 23 chromosomes. Gametes (sperm and eggs) are haploid; they fuse at fertilization to produce a diploid[§48] zygote.

Independent Assortment

§56Define inheritance, gene, allele, homozygous, heterozygous, dominant, recessive, polygenic, pleiotropicExam topic

Inheritance vocabulary

Answer

  • Inheritance — the passing of genetic traits from parents to offspring.
  • Gene — a sequence of DNA that codes for a specific protein (or RNA product); the basic unit of heredity.
  • Allele — an alternative version of a gene. Diploid cells carry two alleles per gene (one on each homologous chromosome).
  • Homozygous — both alleles for a gene are the same (e.g. YY or yy). Homozygous dominant = two dominant alleles; homozygous recessive = two recessive alleles.
  • Heterozygous — one dominant and one recessive allele (e.g. Yy). Heterozygotes can be carriers of recessive disease alleles without showing the trait.
  • Dominant — an allele whose effect appears whenever it is present (one copy is enough).
  • Recessive — an allele whose effect is masked when a dominant allele is present; usually encodes a non-functional protein.
  • Polygenic — a trait controlled by multiple genes (e.g. skin color, height). Produces continuous variation.
  • Pleiotropic — a single gene that has multiple, seemingly unrelated effects on the phenotype (e.g. Marfan syndrome).

§57Explain incomplete dominance — phenotype and genotypeExam topic

Incomplete dominance

Answer

Incomplete dominance: the heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype between the two homozygotes — neither allele fully masks the other.

Classic example: red snapdragon (R₁R₁) × white snapdragon (R₂R₂) → all pink F1 (R₁R₂). Cross two pinks → 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white.

Mechanism: the recessive allele still encodes a non-functional protein. With only one functional copy (heterozygote), the cell makes ~half as much pigment, producing the intermediate color.

Genotype is still 1:2:1 (RR : Rr : rr); the difference from complete dominance is that all three genotypes have distinct phenotypes, so phenotype ratio = genotype ratio.

§59How are alleles distributed into gametes? If a parent is Pp...Exam topic

How are alleles distributed into gametes?

Answer

During meiosis I, the two members of a homologous pair separate, so each gamete receives only one of the two alleles for any given gene. - A homozygous parent (PP or pp) can only make one kind of gamete: P or p, respectively. - A heterozygous parent (Pp) makes two kinds of gametes in equal proportions — half carry P, half carry p.

This is the physical basis of Mendel's Law of Segregation[§68]: the two alleles for one gene end up in separate gametes.

§60How can the environment regulate phenotype?Exam topic

How does the environment regulate phenotype?

Answer

Many genes have phenotypic effects that depend on environmental conditions (temperature, nutrition, etc.). The genotype is fixed, but expression varies.

Classic example — Siamese cat fur color: the enzyme that produces dark pigment is only active at cool temperatures. The cat's body core is too warm for the enzyme to work, so the trunk stays light, but the cooler extremities (ears, paws, tail, face) develop dark fur.

Other examples: hydrangea flower color (depends on soil pH), human height (genetics + nutrition), X-Inactivation[§91] in calico cats (random, but environmental position effect during development).

§61How does crossing over create new patterns of gene linkage?Exam topic

How does crossing over break up gene linkage?

Answer

Linked genes are usually inherited together — but during prophase I of meiosis, Crossing Over[§18] swaps DNA segments between homologous chromosomes. If a crossover happens between two linked genes, it produces recombinant chromosomes that carry a new combination of alleles (one from each parental chromosome).

The probability of crossing over between two genes is proportional to the distance between them — genes far apart cross over often (looking nearly unlinked), while genes very close together cross over rarely (looking tightly linked). Geneticists exploit this to map gene positions.

§63Relationship between P, F1, and F2 generationsExam topic

P, F1, and F2 generations

Answer

  • P generation (parental) — the original true-breeding parents Mendel started with (e.g. YY × yy).
  • F1 generation (first filial) — the hybrid offspring of the P cross. With one dominant and one recessive parent, all F1 are heterozygous (Yy) and show the dominant phenotype.
  • F2 generation — produced when F1 individuals self-pollinate (Yy × Yy). F2 shows a 3:1 phenotypic ratio of dominant : recessive (1 YY : 2 Yy : 1 yy genotype ratio).

The reappearance of the recessive trait in F2 is what convinced Mendel that "factors" (alleles) are inherited in discrete units — the basis of Mendel's Law of Segregation[§68].

§64Understand the law of independent assortment (Ch 10)Exam topic

Law of independent assortment (in Mendelian inheritance)

Answer

The alleles for one gene segregate into gametes independently of the alleles for another gene.

Mendel's exam-only requirement: understand the principle, but the exam will only ask about single alleles (no dihybrid cross math expected). The cellular basis is metaphase I of meiosis — homologous pairs orient randomly relative to one another, so a Y allele from one chromosome is just as likely to end up with R as with r from a different chromosome.

Caveat: only true for genes on different chromosomes (or far apart on the same chromosome). Genes that are close together on the same chromosome are linked and tend to be inherited together — see What is gene linkage?[§66].

Gene linkage: genes that sit close together on the same chromosome tend to be inherited as a unit — they don't sort independently. The closer they are, the more likely they are inherited together.

§67Why did Mendel see phenotypic and genotypic differences in F1 and F2?Exam topic

What did Mendel observe and why?

Answer

Mendel crossed two true-breeding parents differing in one trait (e.g. purple-flowered PP × white-flowered pp).

F1 observation: all offspring showed the dominant trait (all purple). Why? Each parent contributed one allele; F1 are heterozygous (Pp), and the dominant allele masks the recessive.

F2 observation: when F1 self-pollinated (Pp × Pp), the recessive trait reappeared in roughly 3:1 dominant:recessive (e.g. 705 purple to 224 white). Why? Each Pp parent makes two kinds of gametes (P or p) in equal numbers; the four possible offspring combinations (PP, Pp, Pp, pp) yield genotype ratio 1:2:1 and phenotype ratio 3:1.

Principle: alleles segregate into separate gametes during meiosis (Mendel's Law of Segregation[§68]), and they recombine randomly at fertilization. A recessive allele is not "diluted" — it can hide in a heterozygote and reappear in later generations.

§68Mendel's Law of SegregationPrinciple

Law of Segregation (Mendel's First Law)

Summary

The two alleles for a gene segregate (separate) into different gametes during meiosis. Each gamete therefore receives only one of the two alleles. At fertilization, the random combination of male and female gametes restores the diploid pair.

Cellular mechanism: at anaphase I of meiosis[§16], the homologous chromosomes (carrying the two alleles) separate to opposite poles.

Heterozygous parents (Yy) make two kinds of gametes (Y and y) in equal proportion. This explains the 3:1 phenotypic ratio in F2 generations.

§69Mendel's Pea Plant ExperimentsExperiment

Mendel's Pea Plant Experiments

Summary

Gregor Mendel crossed true-breeding pea plants for several traits (seed color, seed shape, flower color, etc.) over multiple generations and counted phenotype ratios. From these careful tallies he inferred:

  1. Traits are inherited as discrete factors (now called alleles), not blended fluids.
  2. Each parent contributes one factor per trait to each offspring (the other comes from the second parent).
  3. The two factors separate (segregate) into different gametes — Mendel's Law of Segregation[§68].
  4. Different traits assort independently of each other — Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment[§15].

His 1866 paper went unappreciated until 1900, when independent rediscovery by de Vries, Correns, and Tschermak launched modern genetics.

What Mendel Saw

  • True-breeding parents (P generation) → uniform F1, all showing dominant trait.
  • F1 self-cross → F2 with 3:1 ratio of dominant : recessive, confirming that the recessive factor was preserved (not lost) in F1.

  • chapter-10-inheritance slides 9–22

§70Punnett SquareTechnique

Punnett Square

Summary

A grid that uses the genotypes of two parents to predict the possible genotypes of their offspring and their relative proportions.

How to use (monohybrid example, Tt × Tt): 1. Write one parent's gametes across the top (T, t) and the other's down the side (T, t). 2. Fill each cell by combining the row and column letters → TT, Tt, Tt, tt. 3. Count: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt → 3:1 phenotypic ratio (dominant : recessive).

Sex-linked variant: the X-bearing gametes carry one allele, but the Y-bearing male gamete has no allele — leading to the asymmetric inheritance patterns of X-Linked Recessive[§76] disorders.

The exam will only require single-allele (monohybrid) Punnett squares, including X-linked.

§71CodominanceInheritance Pattern

Codominance

Summary

A non-Mendelian inheritance pattern in which both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygote — neither masks the other. Both proteins are produced and both phenotypes are visible simultaneously (not blended).

Classic example: ABO blood typing. The I^A and I^B alleles are codominant — heterozygotes (I^A I^B) have both A and B antigens on their red blood cells, giving blood type AB. See ABO Blood Typing[§84].

§72Incomplete DominanceInheritance Pattern

Incomplete Dominance

Summary

A non-Mendelian inheritance pattern in which the heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype between the two homozygotes. Classic example: red snapdragon × white snapdragon → pink F1. With incomplete dominance, genotype ratio = phenotype ratio (1:2:1) because each genotype has a unique phenotype.

Mechanistically, the recessive allele still encodes a non-functional protein; with only one functional copy, the cell makes about half as much pigment, producing the blended color.

§75Sex-Linked InheritanceInheritance Pattern

Sex-Linked Inheritance

Summary

X-Linked Recessive

Summary

A specific Sex-Linked Inheritance[§75] pattern in which the recessive allele is on the X chromosome. §85Crossover ProbabilityConcept

Crossover Probability

Summary

The probability of Crossing Over[§18] occurring between two genes is proportional to the distance between them on the chromosome. Genes far apart cross over often; closely linked genes cross over rarely. This is the basis for genetic mapping — frequencies of recombination are translated into distances (centimorgans).

§86Dihybrid CrossConcept

Dihybrid Cross

Summary

A genetic cross tracking two genes simultaneously (e.g. seed color × seed shape). For two heterozygous parents (AaBb × AaBb), the F2 generation shows the classic 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio: - 9 dominant–dominant - 3 dominant–recessive - 3 recessive–dominant - 1 recessive–recessive

§87Environment Affects PhenotypeConcept

Environment and Phenotype

Summary

Genotype is fixed; phenotype is genotype × environment. Many genes have phenotypic effects that depend on temperature, nutrition, light, or other environmental conditions.

Classic example — Siamese cat fur: the enzyme that produces dark pigment is only active at cool temperatures. The cat's body core is too warm, so the trunk stays light; the cooler extremities (ears, paws, tail, face) develop dark fur.

Other examples: - Hydrangea flower color depends on soil pH. - Human height depends on genes + nutrition. - Skin color (polygenic) is also affected by sun exposure.

§88EpistasisConcept

Epistasis

Summary

A gene-interaction effect: one gene's product affects the expression of another gene. Epistasis can mask Mendelian ratios — for example, if gene B is required to "see" the effect of gene A, then the F2 ratios deviate from the expected 9:3:3:1 because some genotypes can't show their A phenotype without B.

A classic example is mouse coat color, where one gene controls pigment production and a second controls pigment distribution.

  • chapter-10-inheritance slide 60

§89Gene LinkageConcept

Gene Linkage

Summary

Genes located close together on the same chromosome are linked — they tend to be inherited together rather than assorting independently. This is an exception to Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment[§15].

How tightly two genes are linked depends on distance: the closer they are, the less likely a Crossing Over[§18] event will separate them. Geneticists use crossover frequency (in centimorgans) to map gene positions.

Monohybrid Cross

Summary

A genetic cross that tracks one gene (one trait) at a time, typically Aa × Aa between two heterozygotes. The classic monohybrid cross produces a 3:1 phenotypic ratio in F2 (dominant : recessive) and a 1:2:1 genotype ratio (AA : Aa : aa).

§91X-InactivationConcept

X-Inactivation

Summary

A mechanism for dosage compensation: in each cell of an XX individual, one of the two X chromosomes is randomly silenced ("inactivated") early in development and condensed into a Barr body. This prevents double-dosing of X-linked gene products.

Random inactivation means each cell expresses different alleles. In calico cats, this produces patches of orange and black fur where each patch descends from a cell that inactivated one or the other X. In humans, X-inactivation explains the variable expression of Rett syndrome[§82] in girls — the more cells that silence the Rett-carrying X, the milder the disease.

§92CarrierVocabulary Term

Carrier

Summary

A heterozygous individual who carries a recessive disease allele but does not show the disease. Two carriers (Aa × Aa) have a 25% chance of having an affected child (aa). For X-Linked Recessive[§76] traits, only females can be carriers — males have only one X, so any X-linked recessive allele is expressed.

§93Dominant AlleleVocabulary Term

Dominant

Summary

An allele whose effect appears whenever it is present — even in heterozygotes (Yy shows the dominant phenotype). Mendel called this trait the "dominant" of the pair. Dominant alleles typically encode functional proteins, whereas recessive alleles often encode non-functional or absent products.

§94F1 GenerationVocabulary Term

F1 Generation

Summary

The first filial generation — the offspring of the P cross. From a YY × yy cross, all F1 are heterozygous (Yy) and show the dominant phenotype.

§95F2 GenerationVocabulary Term

F2 Generation

Summary

The second filial generation — produced by self-pollinating F1 individuals (Yy × Yy). The F2 shows the classic 3:1 phenotypic ratio (dominant : recessive) and 1:2:1 genotype ratio (YY : Yy : yy). The reappearance of the recessive trait in F2 was Mendel's key clue that traits are inherited as discrete units (alleles), not blended.

§96GeneVocabulary Term

Gene

Summary

A sequence of DNA that encodes a specific protein (or a functional RNA, like tRNA/rRNA). The basic unit of heredity. Genes occupy specific positions (loci[§101]) on chromosomes; diploid cells have two copies of each gene (one on each homologous chromosome) which may be the same or different alleles[§46].

§97GenotypeVocabulary Term

Genotype

Summary

An organism's two alleles for a given gene (or set of genes). Written with capital letters for dominant alleles and lowercase for recessive (e.g. YY, Yy, yy). Genotype determines phenotype[§103], but two organisms with the same phenotype may have different genotypes (homozygous dominant vs. heterozygous both look dominant).

§98HeterozygousVocabulary Term

Heterozygous

Summary

Carrying one dominant and one recessive allele for a given gene (e.g. Yy). Heterozygotes can be carriers[§92] of recessive disease alleles — they don't show the disease but can pass the recessive allele to offspring.

§99HomozygousVocabulary Term

Homozygous

Summary

Carrying two identical alleles for a given gene. Subtypes: - Homozygous dominant (e.g. YY) — two dominant alleles. - Homozygous recessive (e.g. yy) — two recessive alleles; this is when a recessive trait is expressed.

True-breeding[§107] organisms are homozygous for the gene of interest.

§100InheritanceVocabulary Term

Inheritance

Summary

The passing of genetic traits from parents to offspring. The molecular basis is the transmission of DNA via gametes (sperm and eggs). Patterns of inheritance — Mendelian, codominant, sex-linked, polygenic, etc. — describe how alleles segregate and combine across generations.

§101LocusVocabulary Term

Locus

Summary

The specific physical location of a gene on a chromosome. Each gene has its own locus. The two homologous chromosomes carry the same gene at the same locus, possibly in different allele forms.

§102P GenerationVocabulary Term

P Generation

Summary

The parental generation in a Mendelian cross — the two true-breeding parents that start the experiment (e.g. YY × yy). Their offspring are the F1 generation.

§103PhenotypeVocabulary Term

Phenotype

Summary

The observable characteristics of an organism (color, shape, behavior, biochemistry). Phenotype is the result of genotype + environment — see Environment Affects Phenotype[§87] (Siamese cat fur color is the classic example). For a gene with complete dominance, both YY and Yy give the same phenotype but different genotypes.

§104PleiotropyVocabulary Term

Pleiotropy

Summary

When one gene has multiple, seemingly unrelated effects on the phenotype. The textbook example is Marfan syndrome: a mutation in fibrillin-1 affects connective tissue and produces tall stature, long fingers, lens dislocation, aortic aneurysm, etc. — many unrelated symptoms from one gene.

§105PolygenicVocabulary Term

Polygenic

Summary

A trait controlled by multiple genes — each contributing a small effect. Polygenic traits show continuous variation across a population (e.g. skin color, height, weight) rather than discrete categories. Often called "continuous" traits. See Polygenic Trait[§74].

§106Recessive AlleleVocabulary Term

Recessive

Summary

An allele whose effect is masked when a dominant allele is also present. Recessive traits appear only in homozygous-recessive individuals (yy). Recessive alleles typically encode non-functional proteins. Many genetic diseases (cystic fibrosis, sickle cell, albinism) are autosomal-recessive.

§107True BreedingVocabulary Term

True Breeding (Pure Breeding)

Summary

A line of organisms whose self-fertilized offspring all show the same trait as the parent. True-breeding individuals are homozygous[§99] for the gene of interest. "Pure breeding" = "true breeding" — they're synonymous. Mendel started his experiments by establishing true-breeding lines.

§109Chargaff's rule — if 20% of a sequence is T, what are the others?Exam topic

Chargaff's rule

Answer

Chargaff's rule: in any double-stranded DNA, %A = %T and %G = %C (because A always pairs with T and G always pairs with C). Therefore %A + %T + %G + %C = 100%.

Worked example — 20% T: - %T = 20% → %A = 20% (Chargaff) - %A + %T = 40% → %G + %C = 60% → %G = %C = 30% each

So: A = 20%, T = 20%, G = 30%, C = 30%.

§111Direction of template reading and new-strand synthesis; what nucleotide part determines directionality?Exam topic

§112Four nucleotides in DNA and how they pairExam topic

Four nucleotides in DNA and base-pairing rules

Answer

DNA contains four nitrogenous bases: - Adenine (A) — pairs with Thymine (T) via 2 hydrogen bonds - Guanine (G) — pairs with Cytosine (C) via 3 hydrogen bonds

A and G are purines (double ring); T and C are pyrimidines (single ring). A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine, keeping the helix the same width.

In RNA, uracil (U) replaces thymine, so RNA uses A-U and G-C pairing.

§114Given a DNA sequence, can you write the complementary strand?Exam topic

§115Is DNA replication different in cells undergoing mitosis vs. meiosis?Exam topic

DNA replication: mitosis vs. meiosis

Answer

No — the replication mechanism is the same. In both cases, DNA is replicated once during S phase of interphase, using the same enzymes (helicase, primase, DNA polymerase III, etc.) and producing two identical sister chromatids per chromosome.

The difference is in what happens after: - Mitosis — one round of division separates sister chromatids → 2 diploid cells. - Meiosis — two rounds of division (no replication between them); meiosis I separates homologues, meiosis II separates sister chromatids → 4 haploid cells.

§117What are leading and lagging strands? Why are they synthesized differently?Exam topic

Leading vs. lagging strand

Answer

DNA polymerase III can only add nucleotides to the 3' end (synthesis proceeds 5' → 3'). But the two template strands are antiparallel, so as the replication fork opens, only one of them can be read continuously toward the fork.

  • Leading strand — synthesized continuously in the 5' → 3' direction toward the replication fork. One primer, one long DNA strand.
  • Lagging strand — must be synthesized away from the replication fork to keep the same 5' → 3' direction. So it's built in short pieces called Okazaki fragments, each starting with its own RNA primer. DNA polymerase I later replaces the primers, and DNA ligase joins the fragments.

Why differently: the antiparallel orientation of the two strands plus the 5' → 3' rule for DNA polymerase makes simultaneous continuous synthesis on both strands physically impossible.

§118What happens to telomere length as we age?Exam topic

Telomere length and aging

Answer

Telomeres shorten with age. Most somatic (body) cells lack telomerase[§123], so each round of cell division clips a bit off the end of each chromosome. Eventually telomeres reach a critical minimum length and the cell stops dividing (cellular senescence) — Greider proposed that the maximum number of cell divisions is set by the initial telomere length. In cell culture, telomere length and division count correlate.

This is one molecular mechanism that contributes to aging at the cellular level.

What is a mutagen?

Answer

A mutagen is any agent (chemical, radiation, or biological) that increases the rate of mutations in DNA above the spontaneous background rate. Examples: UV light, X-rays, tobacco smoke, certain industrial chemicals, some viruses.

Mutagens can damage bases, cause cross-linking between strands, or insert themselves between bases — all of which lead to mistakes during the next round of DNA replication. Most mutations occur during replication itself; mutagens dramatically increase that error rate.

§120What is a telomere and why is replicating it difficult?Exam topic

Telomeres and the replication problem

Answer

A telomere is a region of repetitive DNA sequence (in humans: TTAGGG repeats) at the ends of linear chromosomes. Telomeres protect the chromosome from being read as broken DNA and from being degraded.

Why replicating the end is difficult: the lagging strand needs an RNA primer to start each Okazaki fragment. At the very end of the chromosome, after the last RNA primer is removed, there's no upstream 3'-OH for DNA polymerase to extend onto, so the gap can't be filled. The chromosome shortens by a small amount with each round of replication.

This problem only affects the lagging strand of linear chromosomes. Prokaryotes don't have it (their DNA is circular).

§122What is complementary base pairing? Complement of 5'-CGCATGTCAGC-3'?Exam topic

Complementary base pairing — and a worked example

Answer

Complementary base pairing: A pairs with T (2 H-bonds), G pairs with C (3 H-bonds). Each base only pairs with its specific partner, so one strand of DNA dictates the sequence of the other.

The two strands are also antiparallel — one runs 5'→3', the other runs 3'→5'.

Worked example — find the complement of 5'-CGCATGTCAGC-3'.

Original (5' → 3') C G C A T G T C A G C
Complement (3' → 5') G C G T A C A G T C G

So the complementary strand written 3' → 5' is 3'-GCGTACAGTCG-5', or equivalently 5'-GCTGACATGCG-3' when written in the conventional 5' → 3' direction.

§123What is telomerase?Exam topic

What is telomerase?

Answer

Telomerase is an enzyme that adds repetitive DNA sequence to the 3' end of telomeres, compensating for the shortening that occurs each round of replication. Telomerase carries its own RNA template that it uses as a guide.

Where it's active: - Germ cells (so eggs and sperm have full-length telomeres) - Stem cells - Cancer cells (~85% of human cancers reactivate telomerase, allowing unlimited division)

Where it's normally absent: most adult somatic cells — which is why their telomeres shorten with age and why most body cells have a finite division limit.

§126Chargaff's RulePrinciple

Chargaff's Rule

Summary

In any double-stranded DNA: %A = %T and %G = %C. So %A + %T + %G + %C = 100%, and %A + %T = 100% − (%G + %C).

This is a direct consequence of Complementary Base Pairing[§127] — every A on one strand has a T paired with it on the other, and every G has a C. Erwin Chargaff discovered this empirically in the 1940s; it provided crucial evidence for Watson and Crick's double helix.

Worked Example

If T = 20%, then A = 20%; G + C must total 60%, so G = C = 30% each.

§127Complementary Base PairingPrinciple

Complementary Base Pairing

Summary

The principle that DNA bases pair specifically and exclusively: - A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds) in DNA; A pairs with U in RNA - G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)

Each strand of DNA therefore dictates the sequence of its partner — a sequence on one strand uniquely defines the complement.

This principle is the basis of: - DNA Replication[§131] — new strand is complementary to template - Transcription[§195] — RNA is complementary to template DNA - Translation[§199] — anticodon is complementary to codon - Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)[§243] — primers anneal to complementary regions

§128Semiconservative ReplicationPrinciple

Semiconservative Replication

Summary

When DNA replicates, each daughter helix has one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand. This is the model proposed by Watson and Crick and confirmed by the Meselson–Stahl experiment (1958).

Contrasts with two alternatives: - Conservative — original double helix stays together; new copy is entirely new. - Dispersive — both daughter helices are mosaics of old and new pieces.

The semiconservative mechanism is built into the way DNA polymerase uses each parental strand as a template.

§129Griffith's Bacterial Transformation ExperimentExperiment

Griffith's Experiment (1928)

Summary

Frederick Griffith worked with two strains of Streptococcus pneumoniae: - Smooth (S) strain — virulent (kills mice) - Rough (R) strain — harmless

He observed: 1. Live S → mouse dies 2. Live R → mouse lives 3. Heat-killed S → mouse lives (confirms S is dead) 4. Heat-killed S + live R → mouse dies, and live S bacteria are recovered from the dead mouse

Conclusion: something from the dead S bacteria transformed the harmless R bacteria into the virulent type. That "something" — later identified as DNA by Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty in 1944 — is the molecule of heredity.

§130Cell CycleProcesse

Cell Cycle

Summary

The ordered sequence of events a cell goes through from one division to the next. Composed of interphase (G1 → S → G2) followed by M phase (mitosis or meiosis).

Phases

  1. G1 — first gap; cell grows, performs normal functions
  2. S phase — DNA replication (DNA Replication[§131]); each chromosome becomes two sister chromatids
  3. G2 — second gap; cell finishes growing, prepares for division
  4. M phase — Mitosis[§24] or Meiosis[§19] + cytokinesis

DNA replication occurs only during S phase, regardless of whether the cell is heading into mitosis or meiosis.

§131DNA ReplicationProcesse

DNA Replication

Summary

The process of copying the entire DNA of a cell before division. Replication is semiconservative[§128]: each daughter helix has one original (template) strand and one newly synthesized strand. Occurs during S Phase[§132] of interphase.

Mechanism

  1. DNA Helicase[§156] unwinds the double helix at the Origin of Replication[§140], creating a Replication Fork[§145].
  2. Single-Stranded Binding Protein (SSB)[§162] keeps the single strands separated.
  3. Topoisomerase[§164] relieves twisting tension ahead of the fork.
  4. RNA Primase[§161] lays down a short RNA primer with a free 3'-OH.
  5. DNA Polymerase III[§160] extends the primer 5' → 3', adding nucleotides complementary to the template (A-T, G-C).
  6. The Leading Strand[§137] is synthesized continuously toward the fork. The Lagging Strand[§136] is synthesized away from the fork in Okazaki fragments[§139].
  7. DNA Polymerase I[§159] replaces RNA primers with DNA.
  8. DNA Ligase[§157] seals the gaps between fragments.

Key Properties

  • Direction of synthesis: 5' → 3' (template read 3' → 5')
  • Speed: bacteria ~1000 nt/sec; eukaryotes ~50 nt/sec
  • Accuracy after proofreading: ~1 mistake per 10⁷ nucleotides
  • Prokaryotes use one Origin of Replication[§140] on a circular chromosome; eukaryotes use many on linear chromosomes (Prokaryote vs. Eukaryote DNA Replication[§166])
  • The lagging strand of linear chromosomes can't replicate to the very end → telomere[§147] shortening (rescued by What is telomerase?[§123] in some cells)

§133Antiparallel StrandsStructure

Antiparallel Strands

Summary

The two DNA strands run in opposite 5' → 3' directions. One strand goes 5' → 3' "up" while the other goes 5' → 3' "down" relative to it.

This matters because DNA polymerase[§160] can only build new strands in the 5' → 3' direction. With antiparallel templates, only one of the two strands can be synthesized continuously toward a replication fork (Leading Strand[§137]); the other (Lagging Strand[§136]) is built in fragments.

  • 5' end — has a free phosphate group on the 5' carbon of the sugar.
  • 3' end — has a free –OH on the 3' carbon of the sugar.

§134Double HelixStructure

Double Helix

Summary

The 3D structure of DNA: two antiparallel polynucleotide strands wound around a common axis like a twisted ladder. Discovered by Watson and Crick (1953, building on Rosalind Franklin's X-ray data).

  • Sides of the ladder — two sugar–phosphate backbones[§146] (exterior, hydrophilic).
  • Rungs of the ladder — pairs of nitrogenous bases[§138] held together by hydrogen bonds[§135] (interior).
  • The two strands run antiparallel: one 5' → 3', the other 3' → 5'.

Each turn of the helix is ~3.4 nm (10 base pairs).

§135Hydrogen BondStructure

Hydrogen Bond

Summary

The weak non-covalent attraction holding complementary base pairs together inside the Double Helix[§134]. A–T pairs share 2 H-bonds; G–C pairs share 3 H-bonds, so G–C-rich regions are slightly harder to separate. Hydrogen bonds are weak enough to be broken thermally — that's why heat (e.g. 95 °C in PCR[§243] denaturation) separates DNA strands without breaking the covalent backbone.

§136Lagging StrandStructure

Lagging Strand

Summary

The strand that must be synthesized away from the replication fork[§145] because of the 5' → 3' rule. To accomplish this, the lagging strand is built in short pieces called Okazaki fragments[§139]. Each fragment requires its own RNA primer (laid down by RNA Primase[§161]); DNA Polymerase I[§159] later replaces those primers with DNA, and DNA Ligase[§157] seals the gaps.

§170Describe the process of transcription in detailExam topic

Transcription — full process

Answer

Transcription = making an RNA copy of a gene from a DNA template. Three steps:

  1. Initiation — RNA polymerase binds the promoter at the start of a gene; in eukaryotes, transcription factors must be present for binding. RNA polymerase unwinds the DNA double helix locally to expose the template strand.
  2. Elongation — RNA polymerase moves along the template strand, reading 3' → 5' and synthesizing an RNA strand in the 5' → 3' direction. Free RNA nucleotides base-pair with the template (A→U, T→A, G→C, C→G). Only one of the two DNA strands is used as template.
  3. Termination — RNA polymerase reaches the terminator sequence; the new RNA, the DNA, and the polymerase separate. The DNA re-forms a double helix; the RNA leaves to be processed.

In eukaryotes, the new transcript is a pre-mRNA: a 5' cap and 3' poly-A tail are added, and introns are spliced out (only exons remain in the mature mRNA). The mature mRNA then exits the nucleus to be translated.

§171Describe translation in detail (and how it differs from transcription)Exam topic

Translation — full process

Answer

Translation = synthesizing a protein from an mRNA template at a ribosome. Three steps:

  1. Initiation — Small ribosomal subunit binds the 5' cap of mRNA and scans for the start codon (AUG). The initiator tRNA, carrying methionine, binds with its anticodon to the AUG. Then the large ribosomal subunit joins, completing the ribosome.
  2. Elongation — A second tRNA (carrying the next amino acid) enters the A site next to the initiator tRNA in the P site. The ribosome (rRNA enzymatic activity) catalyzes a peptide bond between the two amino acids. The first tRNA exits; the ribosome shifts one codon to the right, moving the second tRNA to the P site and freeing the A site for the next tRNA. Repeat for each codon.
  3. Termination — When the ribosome reaches a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA), no tRNA matches it. Instead, a release factor binds and triggers release of the finished polypeptide and dissociation of the ribosome subunits.

Differences from transcription: - Template is mRNA (not DNA). - Reads in groups of 3 (codons), producing amino acids (not nucleotides). - Uses tRNA as adaptors and rRNA for catalysis at ribosomes. - Happens in the cytoplasm (or rough ER) — not the nucleus. - Multiple ribosomes can translate the same mRNA simultaneously (polyribosome).

§175Mechanisms eukaryotes use to regulate gene expressionExam topic

Eukaryotic gene regulation — multiple levels

Answer

Protein synthesis is energetically expensive, so cells regulate it tightly. Eukaryotes regulate at many levels:

  1. Chromatin level — DNA is wrapped tightly around histones. Tightly wound regions (heterochromatin) cannot be transcribed; loosely wound regions (euchromatin) are accessible.
  2. Transcription factors — proteins that must bind to a gene before RNA polymerase can transcribe it. Different cell types have different transcription factors active, so they express different genes.
  3. Alternative splicing — same pre-mRNA, different combinations of exons → different proteins from one gene.
  4. mRNA export — proteins can hold mRNA in the nucleus, preventing it from reaching ribosomes.
  5. mRNA stability — some mRNAs are quickly degraded; others persist. microRNA (short non-coding RNA) silences specific mRNAs by binding to them and blocking translation or causing degradation.
  6. Post-translational — proteins must be folded correctly and routed to the right cellular location. Cells can chemically modify proteins (phosphorylation, etc.) to activate, inactivate, or destroy them.

§176Pre-mRNA — cap, tail, and longer than neededExam topic

Pre-mRNA processing

Answer

The initial transcript made by RNA polymerase is called pre-mRNA (or primary transcript). It is longer than the final message because it contains both introns and exons, and because additional features are added during processing:

  1. 5' cap — a modified guanine nucleotide added to the 5' end. Helps the ribosome recognize the mRNA and protects it from degradation.
  2. 3' poly-A tail — a long string of adenines (~100–250) added to the 3' end. Stabilizes the mRNA and helps it leave the nucleus.
  3. Splicing — introns are excised, exons joined.

The processed product is mature mRNA, which is shorter than the pre-mRNA and ready to be translated in the cytoplasm.

§178Three ways RNA differs from DNAExam topic

Three ways RNA differs from DNA

Answer

  1. Sugar: RNA uses ribose; DNA uses deoxyribose (which lacks one –OH at the 2' position).
  2. Base: RNA uses uracil (U) in place of thymine (T). (A still pairs with U; G still pairs with C.)

A bonus difference: RNA molecules are usually much shorter than chromosomal DNA, and many types exist (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA, microRNA, etc.).

§180What are introns and exons? Which leave the nucleus?Exam topic

Introns and exons

Answer

  • Exons = "expressed" segments — they stay in the mature mRNA and code for the protein.
  • Introns = "intervening" segments — they're transcribed into pre-mRNA but spliced out in the nucleus and never leave as part of the message.

§182What happens during transcription elongation? How many DNA strands are copied?Exam topic

Transcription elongation

Answer

During elongation, RNA polymerase moves along the template strand of DNA and builds an RNA copy by adding nucleotides to the 3' end of the growing RNA strand (synthesis is 5' → 3', just like DNA replication). Each incoming RNA nucleotide pairs with its complementary base on the template (A-U, T-A, G-C, C-G).

Only one of the two DNA strands is copied — the template strand. The other (coding/sense) strand is not transcribed in that gene. Different genes can use different strands as their template.

The resulting RNA is complementary to the template strand, so its sequence matches the coding DNA strand (with U instead of T).

§184What occurs at translation termination? What determines an ending point?Exam topic

Translation termination

Answer

Termination is triggered when the ribosome reaches a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) on the mRNA. No tRNA carries an anticodon for stop codons, so no amino acid is delivered. Instead:

  • A release factor protein binds to the empty A site at the stop codon.
  • The release factor causes the finished polypeptide to be released from the tRNA in the P site.
  • The ribosome subunits dissociate from the mRNA.

The mRNA can then be re-translated by another ribosome, or eventually degraded.

The ending point is determined by the stop codon — the genetic code itself encodes "stop here."

§185What occurs during translation elongation? Function of the second tRNA, peptide bond, etc.Exam topic

Translation elongation

Answer

At the start of elongation, the initiator tRNA with methionine sits in the ribosome's P site, base-paired with the AUG start codon.

  1. Second tRNA arrives — a tRNA carrying the second amino acid enters the A site of the ribosome. Its anticodon must complementary base-pair with the next codon on the mRNA.
  2. Peptide bond formation — the catalytic site of the ribosome (rRNA — peptidyl transferase activity) transfers the methionine from the first tRNA onto the second tRNA, forming a peptide bond between the two amino acids. The growing protein is now attached to the second tRNA.
  3. Translocation (shift) — the first tRNA (now empty) is released from the E site. The ribosome shifts one codon down the mRNA, moving the second tRNA from A site → P site. The A site is now empty and ready for the third tRNA.
  4. Repeat for every codon — each time adding one amino acid to the chain.

The catalytic site is part of the large ribosomal subunit (rRNA, not protein, does the catalysis — ribosomes are ribozymes).

§188Central Dogma of Molecular BiologyPrinciple

Central Dogma

Summary

The flow of genetic information in cells:

DNA → RNA → Protein

  • DNA → RNA: Transcription[§195] (in the nucleus) catalyzed by RNA Polymerase[§220]
  • RNA → Protein: Translation[§199] (at ribosomes in the cytoplasm) catalyzed by the Ribosome[§208]

Originally articulated by Francis Crick. Modern revisions include reverse transcription (RNA → DNA, e.g. retroviruses) and RNA replication, but the core flow holds.

§189Codon RedundancyPrinciple

Codon Redundancy

Summary

Most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon. With 64 codons but only 20 amino acids, this redundancy means many point mutations (especially in the 3rd position of a codon) produce the same amino acid — these are silent mutations with no effect on the protein.

Example: leucine has 6 codons (UUA, UUG, CUU, CUC, CUA, CUG). A 3rd-position substitution within the CUx group typically doesn't change the amino acid.

  • Large subunit — binds tRNAs[§219] in three sites: A (aminoacyl, incoming), P (peptidyl, growing chain), E (exit).

The ribosome's catalytic activity is carried out by rRNA (it's a ribozyme), specifically the peptidyl transferase center in the large subunit, which forms peptide bonds between amino acids.

Multiple ribosomes can simultaneously translate the same mRNA — a structure called a polyribosome.

§221Sickle Cell AnemiaDiseases / Disorder

Sickle Cell Anemia

Summary

The textbook example of a single-base substitution mutation (missense) producing major disease. In the gene encoding β-hemoglobin:

GAG → GTG (a single A→T substitution) changes the 6th amino acid from glutamate (Glu) to valine (Val).

Valine is hydrophobic where glutamate was negatively charged. Under low oxygen, the mutated hemoglobin molecules stick together, deforming red blood cells into rigid sickle shapes that block capillaries. Causes pain crises, organ damage, and reduced oxygen delivery.

Inheritance: autosomal recessive. Heterozygotes have sickle cell trait (mostly asymptomatic) and a survival advantage against malaria, which is why the allele is maintained at high frequency in malaria-prevalent regions.

Most amino acids are encoded by more than one codon — there are 64 codons but only 20 amino acids. §226Frameshift MutationConcept

Frameshift Mutation

Summary

An insertion[§228] or deletion[§225] of a number of bases not a multiple of 3. Because codons are read in groups of three, the reading frame shifts after the mutation site, and every codon downstream changes. The protein after the shift is essentially gibberish, and a premature stop codon often appears soon after — producing a truncated, non-functional protein. Usually catastrophic.

§227Gene RegulationConcept

Gene Regulation

Summary

The control of when, where, and how much a gene is expressed. Eukaryotes regulate at multiple levels: 1. Chromatin — DNA wound on histones; tightly wound = silent. 2. Transcription factors — required for RNA polymerase to bind promoter. 3. Alternative splicing — different exon combinations → different proteins. 4. mRNA export — proteins can hold mRNA in the nucleus. 5. mRNA stability — quick degradation; microRNA silencing. 6. Post-translational — folding, modifications, targeted degradation.

Regulation is essential because protein synthesis is energetically expensive and because different cell types must express different gene sets despite sharing the same genome.

A mutation in which one or more bases are added to the DNA sequence. If it's not a multiple of 3, it causes a frameshift[§226] and is usually catastrophic.

§234Describe the process of gel electrophoresisExam topic

Gel electrophoresis — visualizing PCR products

Answer

Gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size so they can be visualized.

Process: 1. Load DNA (e.g. PCR products) into wells at one end of an agarose gel (a porous polymer). 2. Apply electric current across the gel. DNA's phosphate backbone is negatively charged, so DNA migrates toward the positive electrode. 3. Smaller fragments move faster through the gel mesh; larger fragments move more slowly. Over a fixed time, fragments separate into bands by size. 4. Stain the gel (e.g. with ethidium bromide or SYBR safe) and image under UV light. Each band represents fragments of one size.

For STR profiling, the band pattern is the DNA profile — the positions of bands depend on how many repeats each STR allele has. Comparing profiles from a crime scene to a suspect tells you whether they match.

§235PCR components: DNA, polymerase, free nucleotides, primers — what does each do? What if missing?Exam topic

PCR components and what happens if each is missing

Answer

PCR requires four ingredients (plus heat cycling):

Component Why it's needed Consequence if missing
Target DNA (template) Provides the sequence to be copied Nothing to amplify — no product
DNA polymerase (heat-stable, e.g. Taq) Catalyzes the addition of new nucleotides to the growing strand No new DNA strand can be built
Free nucleotides (dNTPs: dATP, dTTP, dGTP, dCTP) The building blocks that polymerase incorporates into the new strand Polymerase has no raw material — no extension possible
Primers (short single-stranded DNA sequences complementary to the ends of the target) Provide a starting point with a free 3'-OH for polymerase, and define the boundaries of what gets amplified Polymerase can't start — and even if it could, there'd be no specificity to amplify the right region

The mixture is also heat-cycled: ~95 °C (denaturation[§238]) → ~55 °C (annealing[§239]) → ~72 °C (extension[§240]).

§236What is a short tandem repeat? Does expression vary by person?Exam topic

Short tandem repeats (STRs)

Answer

A short tandem repeat (STR) is a stretch of DNA where a short sequence (typically 2–6 base pairs) is repeated head-to-tail many times, e.g. AGAT-AGAT-AGAT-…. The length of the repeat — i.e. how many copies are present — varies between individuals.

Yes, the number of repeats varies dramatically person to person. That's exactly why STRs are useful in DNA profiling: at any one STR locus a person inherits a maternal copy and a paternal copy, each with its own number of repeats.

By analyzing 13 standard STR loci, the FBI's CODIS system creates a DNA profile so distinctive that the chance of two unrelated people matching across all 13 is about one in a trillion. Mismatch on even one STR locus rules out a suspect.

§237What is the purpose of PCR? How many copies after each cycle?Exam topic

Purpose of PCR; doubling per cycle

Answer

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) rapidly amplifies a specific DNA sequence — taking a tiny amount (even a few molecules) and producing billions of copies in a few hours. It's used in forensics, paternity testing, medical diagnostics, genetic engineering, and basic research.

Doubling rule: each PCR cycle doubles the number of target DNA copies. - After cycle 1: 2 copies (2¹) - After cycle 2: 4 copies (2²) - After cycle 3: 8 copies (2³) - After cycle n: 2ⁿ copies

So 30 cycles gives ~10⁹ copies — enough to visualize on a gel.

PCR was invented by Kary Mullis[§245], who won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for it.

§238PCR Step 1 — DenaturationProcesse

PCR — Denaturation

Summary

First step of each Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)[§243] cycle. The mixture is heated to ~95 °C, which breaks the hydrogen bonds between the two strands of DNA and separates them. Hydrogen bonds are easy to disrupt with heat (compared to covalent bonds in the backbone), so heating makes DNA single-stranded without destroying it.

§239PCR Step 2 — AnnealingProcesse

PCR — Annealing

Summary

Second step of each Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)[§243] cycle. The mixture is cooled to ~55 °C so primers[§154] can base-pair (anneal) to their complementary sequences on the now-separated DNA strands. Each primer is short (~20 nt) and is designed to flank one end of the target sequence — one primer binds each strand.

§240PCR Step 3 — ExtensionProcesse

PCR — Extension

Summary

Third step of each Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)[§243] cycle. The temperature is raised to ~72 °C — the optimal temperature for the heat-stable DNA Polymerase[§158] (typically Taq polymerase from a thermophilic bacterium). Polymerase extends the bound primers in the 5' → 3' direction, synthesizing a new copy of the target strand. After extension, the cycle repeats — doubling the copies again.

§241DNA SequencingTechnique

DNA Sequencing

Summary

A technique that determines the exact order of nucleotides in a DNA sample.

Sanger Method (covered in class)

  1. Combine the unknown DNA, primers, free nucleotides, and DNA polymerase. Replication starts as it would in a cell.
  2. Add dye-labeled chain-terminating nucleotides — when one is incorporated, it stops replication and tags the strand with a color.
  3. The result is a mixture of strands of every possible length, each ending in a colored nucleotide.
  4. Run on a gel (Gel Electrophoresis[§242]); shortest fragments travel farthest.
  5. Read the color of the terminal nucleotide at each band → reveals the sequence (complementary to the original template).

Modern fluorescent sequencing automates this; the Human Genome Project (Human Genome Project[§251]) used these technologies to sequence all 3 billion human bases by 2003.

Gel Electrophoresis

§243Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)Technique

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

Summary

A laboratory technique for rapidly amplifying a specific DNA sequence — taking a few molecules and producing billions of copies in a few hours. Invented by Kary Mullis[§245] (1983, Nobel Prize 1993).

Components

  • Target DNA (the template)
  • Heat-stable DNA Polymerase[§158] (e.g. Taq from Thermus aquaticus)
  • dNTPs (free nucleotides as building blocks)
  • Primers — two short DNA sequences that flank the target region

Steps (per cycle)

  1. Denaturation[§238] at ~95 °C — strands separate (H-bonds break)
  2. Annealing[§239] at ~55 °C — primers bind to single-stranded templates
  3. Extension[§240] at ~72 °C — polymerase synthesizes new DNA from primers

Each cycle doubles the target copies. After n cycles: 2ⁿ copies. 30 cycles → ~10⁹ copies — visualizable on a gel.

Applications

Forensics (STR profiling[§244]), paternity testing, infectious disease diagnosis, ancient DNA, gene cloning, COVID testing.

§244STR Analysis (DNA Profiling)Technique

STR Analysis

Summary

A forensic technique that identifies individuals by analyzing short tandem repeats[§254] (STRs) at multiple genomic loci. The standard CODIS panel uses 13 STR loci.

Process

  1. Collect DNA from the crime scene or sample.
  2. Amplify each STR locus using Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)[§243] — each person inherits two copies (one per chromosome) at each locus, possibly with different repeat numbers.
  3. Run on gel electrophoresis[§242] — bands position by length, which depends on repeat count.
  4. Compare profiles — match across all 13 STRs gives a probability of ~1 in a trillion of a random match. Mismatch on even one locus excludes a suspect.

Used to identify the Golden State Killer (Joseph James DeAngelo) and many cold cases.
